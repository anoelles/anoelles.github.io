Washington State Unemployment Services Mobile App

THE CHALLENGE

Design an inclusive and user-friendly mobile app for WA state residents to file their weekly unemployment benefits claims and complete other tasks as required to remain eligible for benefits.

PROJECT BACKGROUND: Washington State residents can file for unemployment benefits by telephone or online. There are no in-person unemployment offices in Washington state.

The fastest way to apply for benefits is online, but currently, 46% of all initial applications are made over the phone, however records show that 94% of all claims could have been completed online.

The current site for filing claims and completing other weekly tasks is not mobile responsive and many clients have expressed their desire to use the site on their mobile phones.

VISION: The WA ESD mobile application will provide job seekers with an efficient and user-friendly way to file their weekly claim, log their required job search efforts, and seek out assistance via an up-to-date FAQ section. Job Seekers will feel at ease with a clearly structured and easy-to-use process with a friendly yet secure interface.

Research

To better understand our users' wants, needs, and motivations, I conducted 13 user interviews, reviewed scholarly journal articles and government data, and reviewed other state government services apps.

SECONDARY RESEARCH: To gain a better understanding of our target user demographics and ensure that our interview sample was representative, I looked up government statistics on unemployment in WA State. I also looked up scholarly articles regarding the effects unemployment on wellbeing in different areas of one’s life to better understand the mindset of users experiencing unemployment.

COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS: We looked at federal and state-level government services apps, as well as financial services apps and health promotion apps.

INTERVIEWS: I first used snowball sampling to identify and interview people who are currently on unemployment. However, we weren’t getting a representative sample and missing input from low-income and younger people of color. I partnered with the local WorkSource office in Seattle, WA to interview clients who were currently on unemployment.

USABILITY OF CURRENT SITE: In order to understand the current claims process, I needed to build enough trust with interviewees that they would allow me to watch them file their weekly claim. 3 interviewees allowed me to observe them filing their weekly claim.

Analysis

I used affinity mapping to analyze interview data, then used all the information our team gathered to create two personas and a user journey to guide our designs.

PERSONAS: Based on our findings, we came up with two personas. The first, and primary user, tends to be lower-income with less social capital and support and lower education levels. This person was usually the primary source of income for their household. The second tends to be from a higher-income background, is college-educated, and has more financial support (from a spouse or parents) to mitigate the effects of being unemployed.

USER JOURNEYIn our interviews, we had clients tell us the story of how they came to be on unemployment and how they ended up on unemployment benefits. Based on this information, we were able to contruct a typical journey that a person takes to get to unemployment services. We also incorporated information on the "5 Stages of Grief" as many of the clients expressed emotions and struggles that were very similar. This helped us to understand the users' mindset at different points of interaction with out app.

Guiding Themes

Based on our research, we came up with core themes to guide our design.

Simplicity

Users wanted the process to be more simple to use than the complicated forms currently provided.

Efficiency

Users were frustrated by having to re-enter the same information over and over again (such as address, phone number) even when nothing had changed.

Mobility

Many users wanted said they would love to be able to file their claim while on public transportation or enter a job activity right after an interview while still at the employer location.

Personal Touch

Users said that the current process feels impersonal and that they feel "like a number rather than a person." They wanted a more personalized experience.

Respect

The poor design on the current site and user flow left people feeling disrespected. As one user stated, "It looks like they don't care at all about the website so I feel like they must not care about me."

Support

Users said they felt like they weren't supported by the current site and process, thus making them lose fatih in the services themselves.

Design and Testing

We used sketching and site-mapping to create our initial design. I created paper-prototypes, and worked with the team to create clickable prototypes to gain user feedback and iterate on our initial designs.

After defining our goals and criteria based on user initial user research, we did ALOT of sketching. We used the design studio process to generate solutions to specific user-identified pain points and used paper-prototyping to get rapid feedback on our early designs.

Our main focus was to keep the research and personas in mind for every design decision.

Example: Many clients initially expressed that they would like a "save" feature to come back and finish their weekly claim later if they didn't get through the whole thing. However, when we tested this feature in our prototype, many were confused by it and most told us that they would never use it. After asking more questions, we discovered that what they really wanted was assurance that their progress was saved if something happened and they couldn't complete. Because of this we added an indicator at the bottom that all progress is auto-saved.

Research Informed Design Decisions

With our final screen mockups, we kept the research in mind and made our design decisions based on actual user information and behavior

Example 1: Being on unemployment and filing their weekly claim often brought up negative emotions of shame, guilt, and stress for clients. We chose soothing colors in our design and used images of nature, which have been shown to have a calming effect of people and may help reduce stress hormone levels.

Example 2: Users were frustrated by having to re-enter information that rarely changed, like their name and address. We designed the weekly claim so that it would save that information, but clients could still review it in the form to insure it's accuracy.

Example 3: Clients rely heavily on the money they receive from submitting their weekly claims, and emphasized that they need to know that the claim was submitted successfully so they can have peace of mind for the next week. We added a large and visible success message after they submit their claim so they can get that confirmation.
